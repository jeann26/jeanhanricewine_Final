During the Goryeo Dynasty, Makgeolli was a drink that could be enjoyed among farmers and working class, when alcohol was segregated by social status
With Korea rapidly modernizing and westernizing due to the Olympics, Makgeolli was no longer the undisputed national drink
Rice was prohibited from being used in alcohol due to rice shortages. This caused a decrease in popularity for makgeolli and more of a demand for soju.
Increase in makgeolli consumption as independent breweries gain more control over production.
With an ammended liquor tax law, small scale brewers could enter the market. Consumer perception has also changed- makgeolli is no longer associated with farmer's brew.

Wash and soak short grain rice for 2-3 hours before steaming.
Allow rice to dry in the sun so the exterior
becomes hard and the inside remains moist.

The key ingredient is to use Nuruk, a wheat
based fermentation starter. It is used in
combination with baker's yeast and water.

Traditionally left to ferment in earthenware
containers for around 9 days, or until no longer
sparkling. Ensure you are gently stirring
everyday 3-4 times a day.

Strain 3-4 times to discard the sludge.
You now have traditional Makgeolli.
